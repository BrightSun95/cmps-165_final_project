Demographic Policy in Xinjiang and
Forced Assimilation of Ethno-religious Minorities

East Turkistan, also known as Xinjiang Uyghur Autonomous Region, is the scene of ongoing conflict between authorities and the indigenous ethnic Uyghur population.

Uyghur separatists claim that East Turkestan is not a part of China, and that it was illegally incorporated into the People’s Republic of China in 1949 and has since been under Chinese occupation.

The population in the region has shifted dramatically from 6% Han Chinese and 75% Uyghur in 1953 to 40% Han and 45% Uyghur in 2010.

This shift in ethnic makeup of the region is the result of policies promoting immigration into Xinjiang as well as the relaxation of population control regulations in the region.

Many believe that the treatment of Uyghurs in the region amounts to genocide, and that population control policies of the past 65 years are a part of the Chinese government’s ongoing effort to forcefully assimilate the Uyghur people.

The goal of this project is to demonstrate the results of these policies, not just on the changes in population, but on the quality of life for Uyghur and other ethno-religious minorities in the region.
